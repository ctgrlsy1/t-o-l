Mammals

2017 - 65 million years ago

About 65 million years ago, after a mass extinction that saw dinosaurs go extinct, mammals began to evolve and diversify from their bird and repile ancestors.

Mammals that evolved during this time include animals that we are familiar with today, including elephants, rabbits, lemurs, and gibbons; as well as our distant cousins, neanderthals, and, eventually, us (humans).

Some of the earliest mammals to evolve after this mass extinction were monotremes - a group of mammals that includes platypus and the long-beaked echidna, humans' 120 millionth cousins.

Birds

~ 320 million years ago

About 320 million years ago, birds evolved from their reptilian (and before that amphibian) ancestors.

Birds continued to live and evolve even after a further three mass extinctions, the most recent of which caused dinosuars to go extinct about 65 million years ago.

Birds belong to the class 'Aves' and, unlike their reptilian ancestors, are endothermic (warm-blooded). Birds are humans' 175 millionth cousins.

Fish

~ 440 million years ago

The first fish evolved from a group of animals called enchinoderms, who themselves evolved from a group of animals called protosomes around 570 million years ago.

Enchinoderms include animals like sea urchins and starfish, who are characteristically unlike other animals in that they have five-sided symmetry (as opposed to bi-lateral symemetry).

Sharks are some of the most ancient fish, evolving from the eel-like hagfish and lancelets.

Protosomes

~ 590 millions years ago

Protosomes incude a diverse range of invertebrates: everything from spiders and butterflies to snails and octopus are protosomes; so called 'mouth-first' because their embryos develop a mouth opening first.

This is in contrast to all vertrebrate animals (including humans) who are called 'deuterostomes'. Deuterostomes develop an opening which forms the anus first, and the mouth second.

Plants

~ 1.35 billion years ago

About 1.35 billion years ago, green algae, one of the first species of plant, evolved from bacteria.

Other plant species, including mosses, ferns, and flowering plants, followed after.

Plants are distinct from animals in that their cells contain chloroplasts which they use to generate energy, as opposed to animals who use mitochondria. They also take in carbon dioxide and excrete oxygren - the opposite of animals.

About 350 million years later, plants gave rise to funghi - humans' 700 millionth cousins.

Bacteria

~ 2.5 billion years ago

About 2 billion years after Earth first formed, bacteria appeared. Bacteria are prokaryotes, or single-celled organisms, meaning that they lack a nucleus and the organelles (Greek for 'little tools') found in the cells of eukaryotes such as plants and animals (organelles like mitochondria and chloroplasts).

Bacteria are ubiqutious and have been found in large quantities all over Earth, including in Earth's crust, acidic hot springs and radioactive waste.

Bacteria also live on other organisms such as plants and animals. Human bodies contain trillions of them (more in fact than the number of cells that make up the totality of the body), forming a parasitic and symbiotic relationship.
